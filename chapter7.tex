\section{Forecasting Performance}
\label{sec:forecasting_performance}

This section evaluates the forecasting behaviour of the proposed dCeNN--ELM backbone in comparison with the two selected baselines, namely Ridge Regression and LSTM. Since the anomaly-detection pipeline developed in this thesis is residual-based, forecasting quality is not an isolated objective; it directly determines the statistical reliability of downstream anomaly generation. For this reason, forecasting performance is assessed at two levels: first, through a benchmark-level comparison across models; and second, through a more detailed examination of target-wise and month-wise prediction behaviour in the 2022 test period \cite{repo_benchmark_comparison,repo_metrics_summary,repo_metrics_monthly}.

A careful interpretation of the forecasting results is required. The purpose of this section is not merely to identify the numerically strongest regressor, but to understand how the proposed architecture behaves relative to the intended design goal of the thesis. In particular, the dCeNN--ELM model is expected to occupy a middle ground between the simplicity of Ridge Regression and the heavier sequential expressiveness of LSTM. The forecasting results therefore need to be interpreted jointly in terms of accuracy, computational efficiency, and suitability as a backbone for the later neuro-symbolic anomaly analysis.

Several observations follow from these results.

First, forecasting difficulty is clearly target-dependent. The renewable capacity-factor targets exhibit relatively small RMSE values in absolute terms because they are normalized variables, whereas load and price show substantially larger absolute errors due to their scale and volatility. This is expected in principle, but the anomaly counts indicate that price and wind are especially challenging from the perspective of residual-based abnormality.

Second, the reported negative $R^2$ values across all four targets indicate that the implemented forecasting backbone, in its committed test-period result snapshot, does not explain variance well relative to a naive mean reference on this evaluation. This is a significant result and should not be obscured. It suggests that the forecasting task as currently configured is non-trivial and that the model residuals should be interpreted not as evidence of perfect predictive fit, but as a noisy basis for anomaly candidate generation that later requires threshold calibration and symbolic refinement.

For this reason, RMSE and MAE are much more informative than MAPE for interpreting renewable forecasting quality in this context.

From the perspective of the overall thesis, these detailed metrics reinforce an important methodological point: the forecasting layer is not presented as a state-of-the-art standalone forecaster. Its role is to provide a structured multivariate expectation model whose residuals can subsequently be refined through thresholding and symbolic reasoning. Accordingly, the forecasting metrics should be read as the first layer of the neuro-symbolic pipeline rather than as the final system objective.

\subsection{Temporal Variability Across the 2022 Test Year}

A major strength of the committed repository outputs is that forecasting quality is also reported month by month in \texttt{metrics\_monthly.csv} \cite{repo_metrics_monthly}. This allows the model to be evaluated not only in aggregate, but also in terms of seasonal variability. Such temporal decomposition is especially important in electricity systems, where weather, renewable generation, load dynamics, and price volatility are strongly season-dependent.

The monthly results show that forecasting difficulty is highly non-uniform across the year. Several patterns are especially notable.

First, \textbf{summer months are the most challenging period for price forecasting}. August 2022 exhibits the highest monthly price RMSE among all reported months, reaching approximately 357.05, with September and July also showing extremely high price error levels \cite{repo_metrics_monthly}. This suggests that the model struggles most in periods of strong market instability, which is plausible given the wider volatility conditions that affected electricity prices in 2022.

Second, \textbf{wind forecasting also deteriorates markedly in summer and early autumn}. Wind RMSE rises above 0.30 in July, August, and September, indicating substantial difficulty in modeling wind-related dynamics during these periods \cite{repo_metrics_monthly}. This is consistent with the later anomaly results, where wind is one of the signals with a high anomaly burden.

Third, \textbf{load forecasting is somewhat more stable than price forecasting but still exhibits substantial month-to-month variation}. The highest monthly load RMSE values appear in the colder and transition months such as March and December, while late summer months such as August and September are somewhat less severe in absolute load RMSE terms \cite{repo_metrics_monthly}. This suggests that the difficulty of load forecasting is driven by a mix of seasonal demand patterns and model mismatch rather than one single regime.

Fourth, \textbf{solar forecasting is affected strongly by low-output periods and seasonal context}. December produces an especially poor $R^2$ value for solar, while other months also show unstable relative error behaviour, again confirming that normalized renewable variables remain challenging when values cluster near zero.

These month-wise results are important because they show that a single global forecasting metric can conceal periods of major instability. For an anomaly-detection framework, this matters directly: anomaly density and threshold behaviour are likely to be affected by the seasonal structure of residuals. The monthly analysis therefore strengthens the case for later threshold calibration and symbolic refinement, especially in the most volatile seasons.

\subsection{Interpretation Relative to the Proposed Thesis Contribution}

The forecasting results should now be interpreted in relation to the actual contribution claim of the thesis. If the thesis were claiming that dCeNN--ELM is the strongest standalone forecaster in absolute statistical terms, the committed benchmark results would not support that claim. Ridge is clearly stronger in the available benchmark table, and the detailed metrics show that forecasting performance remains difficult across several signals and seasons.

However, that is not the most defensible reading of the framework. The more accurate interpretation is that dCeNN--ELM is proposed as a \emph{lightweight nonlinear forecasting backbone} within a larger neuro-symbolic anomaly-analysis system. Under that interpretation, three observations remain strongly relevant.

First, the proposed model preserves a substantial \textbf{efficiency advantage over LSTM}. Its training time and inference latency are much smaller than the sequential deep baseline, which supports the deployment and edge-feasibility arguments developed earlier in the thesis \cite{repo_benchmark_comparison}.

Second, the architecture remains \textbf{structurally compatible with modular refinement}. Its residuals, thresholds, anomaly flags, and later ASP reasoning stages are all cleanly integrated within the repository pipeline. This architectural role is more important than raw RMSE alone for the later neuro-symbolic contribution.

Third, the forecasting difficulty itself helps motivate the symbolic layer. The fact that raw residual behaviour is noisy and seasonally unstable strengthens the need for ASP-based plausibility filtering rather than weakening it. In other words, the current forecasting results make the later anomaly-refinement results more, not less, important.

\subsection{Synthesis}

Overall, the forecasting results paint a mixed but analytically valuable picture. In the committed repository benchmark table, Ridge is the strongest model in terms of raw forecasting accuracy and also the most efficient in training time, while LSTM is the weakest of the three across the main reported dimensions \cite{repo_benchmark_comparison}. The proposed dCeNN--ELM backbone does not dominate on pure RMSE, but it does retain a strong efficiency advantage over LSTM and remains better aligned with the deployment and modularity goals of the full framework.

The monthly evaluation further shows that forecasting difficulty is highly non-uniform across the year, with summer months---especially August---being the most challenging for several targets, particularly price and wind \cite{repo_metrics_monthly}. This leads to an important interpretive stance for the rest of Chapter~7. The value of the proposed system should not be judged solely by whether its forecasts outperform every baseline on every target. Rather, it should be judged by how its forecasting residuals, symbolic refinement, and finance-aware evaluation work together as an integrated anomaly-analysis system. The next section therefore turns from forecasting behaviour to anomaly-detection performance and examines how the ASP veto layer reshapes the raw anomaly set.

\section{Neuro-Symbolic Refinement and False-Alarm Reduction}
\label{sec:ns_refinement_false_alarm_reduction}

This section evaluates the effect of the symbolic refinement layer on the raw anomaly set produced by the forecasting-based detector. In the proposed framework, anomaly generation is deliberately staged. The neural layer first produces raw statistical anomaly candidates from residual exceedance, after which the ASP layer refines this candidate set by enforcing persistence, physical plausibility, and selected market-consistency constraints. The objective of this second stage is not to create anomalies from scratch, but to reduce implausible or weakly supported false alarms before they are passed to downstream interpretation and utility analysis.

This distinction is central to the contribution of the thesis. A purely statistical detector may flag many unusual residuals, but not all of those flags are equally meaningful in domain terms. In a weather-driven electricity setting, some raw anomaly candidates are expected to be spurious, context-dependent, or insufficiently supported by cross-signal evidence. The neuro-symbolic layer therefore acts as a plausibility filter that transforms a broad raw anomaly set into a narrower, more credible refined anomaly set. The key question addressed in this section is thus not only \emph{how many} anomalies were detected, but also \emph{how many of them survived symbolic scrutiny}.

\subsection{Raw Statistical Anomaly Burden}

The raw anomaly burden can be read directly from the committed repository evaluation outputs. According to the global metrics summary for the 2022 test period, the raw statistical detector produced the following anomaly counts: 3502 for solar capacity factor, 4155 for wind capacity factor, 1237 for load, and 4927 for price \cite{repo_metrics_summary}. These values indicate that the raw detector is especially active for price and wind, while load produces substantially fewer anomaly flags.

This pattern is analytically important. Price and wind are also among the most difficult signals in the forecasting evaluation, particularly during volatile months. Their large raw anomaly counts therefore suggest that forecasting instability and contextual uncertainty propagate directly into the raw anomaly set. This is precisely the scenario in which a symbolic plausibility layer becomes valuable: when the raw detector is informative but overly permissive.

To formalize the refinement process, let $N_j^{\mathrm{raw}}$ denote the number of raw anomaly flags for target $j$, and let $N_j^{\mathrm{ref}}$ denote the number of anomalies that remain after ASP refinement. Then the number of eliminated alarms is
\begin{equation}
	N_j^{\mathrm{veto}} = N_j^{\mathrm{raw}} - N_j^{\mathrm{ref}},
	\label{eq:veto_count_ch72}
\end{equation}
and the corresponding retention rate is
\begin{equation}
	R_j = \frac{N_j^{\mathrm{ref}}}{N_j^{\mathrm{raw}}}\times 100.
	\label{eq:retention_rate_ch72}
\end{equation}
These quantities provide the most direct summary of false-alarm reduction in the current neuro-symbolic design.

A more detailed explanation of false-alarm reduction is provided through the report-level veto summary generated by \texttt{src/25\_report\_tables.py} \cite{repo_report_tables}. An important methodological nuance must be stated explicitly: the current repository does \emph{not} store explicit \texttt{veto\_reason} atoms in the committed refined anomaly CSV. These proxy categories are still useful for interpretation, but they should be described honestly as report-level explanatory summaries rather than direct solver-extracted labels.

The repository defines the following main veto-reason categories:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{PV-at-night}, used when solar anomalies occur in time windows consistent with absent radiation;
	\item \textbf{Wind-ramp-guard (proxy)}, used when wind-related anomalies are associated with unusually large short-term changes;
	\item \textbf{Holiday/weekend-exemption (proxy)}, used when load-type anomalies coincide with weekend or holiday-like calendar context;
	\item \textbf{Missing co-anomaly confirmation}, used especially for price anomalies lacking the supporting load anomaly relation;
	\item \textbf{Transient non-persistent spike}, used for isolated or weakly supported anomalies that fail persistence-style confirmation.
\end{itemize}

These categories are highly valuable for the thesis because they move the discussion beyond a generic statement such as ``ASP removed some anomalies.'' Instead, they provide a domain-grounded interpretation of \emph{why} anomalies were removed. In other words, they connect false-alarm reduction to concrete symbolic logic.

\subsection{Interpretation of False-Alarm Reduction}

Taken together, the available repository outputs support a clear interpretation of the symbolic layer. The raw detector is intentionally permissive: it flags a broad candidate set from residual threshold exceedance. The ASP layer then acts as a conservative refinement stage, retaining anomalies that are persistent and contextually plausible while suppressing anomalies that conflict with calendar logic, physical plausibility, or cross-signal confirmation rules.

This means that the neuro-symbolic contribution should not be evaluated by the crude question ``did the system detect more anomalies?'' The more important question is whether the system produced a \emph{better anomaly set}. In the context of this thesis, a better anomaly set is:
\begin{itemize}
	\item smaller where the raw detector is over-sensitive,
	\item more physically and contextually plausible,
	\item more interpretable because veto categories are explainable,
	\item and more useful for the finance-aware downstream layer.
\end{itemize}

This is the sense in which the ASP component contributes to false-alarm reduction. It does not merely reduce anomaly volume; it enforces \emph{qualitative refinement}. That distinction is fundamental and should be emphasized in the final thesis discussion.

\subsection{Synthesis}

The results of this section should be interpreted as evidence that the symbolic layer materially reshapes the anomaly landscape produced by the raw detector. The statistical detector alone yields a large anomaly burden, especially for price and wind. The ASP layer then applies persistence, physical plausibility, and market-consistency constraints in order to prune implausible or weakly supported anomaly candidates. The final outcome is not simply fewer anomalies, but a refined anomaly set that is more defensible in domain terms.

This is a key transition point in the thesis. Section~7.1 showed that forecasting accuracy alone does not fully capture the value of the proposed framework. Section~7.2 extends that argument by showing that anomaly quality depends critically on symbolic refinement. The next section therefore asks the most practically important follow-up question: whether this refined anomaly set also improves downstream financial utility in the simulated market-analysis setting.


\section{Financial Impact of Refined Anomaly Signals}
\label{sec:financial_impact_refined_signals}

The previous section showed that the ASP-based refinement layer reduces implausible or weakly supported anomaly candidates. However, false-alarm reduction is only practically meaningful if it improves downstream usefulness. For that reason, this section evaluates the refined anomaly signals in a finance-aware backtesting setting, where anomaly decisions are translated into utility and penalty outcomes. The central question is therefore not only whether the neuro-symbolic pipeline produces fewer false alarms, but whether it produces anomaly signals that are more valuable when judged through cumulative utility.

This part of the evaluation is important because anomaly detection in isolation can be misleading. A detector that produces many raw alerts may appear highly sensitive, yet still perform poorly if those alerts lead to frequent penalty-bearing decisions. Conversely, a more selective detector may generate higher overall value if it preserves the most actionable events and suppresses low-quality triggers. The proposed neuro-symbolic framework is designed precisely for this trade-off: it narrows the anomaly set in order to improve the utility-to-penalty balance of the final signals.

Within the repository, this financial interpretation is implemented through a finance-mapping stage that converts anomaly outcomes into utility-relevant event streams \cite{repo_finance_mapping}. The resulting backtest outputs are then summarized and visualized through the dedicated utility-comparison script and report-table generation pipeline \cite{repo_utility_comparison,repo_finance_backtest,repo_report_tables}.

\subsection{Utility Formulation}

Let $r_t$ denote the reward or gain associated with a useful anomaly signal at time $t$, and let $p_t$ denote the penalty associated with a false, weak, or otherwise undesirable signal. The net utility at time $t$ is then written as
\begin{equation}
	u_t = r_t - p_t,
	\label{eq:net_utility_ch73}
\end{equation}
and cumulative utility up to time horizon $T$ is
\begin{equation}
	U(T) = \sum_{t=1}^{T} u_t.
	\label{eq:cumulative_utility_ch73}
\end{equation}

To compare the raw neural signal stream with the refined neuro-symbolic stream, the cumulative utility gap can be expressed as
\begin{equation}
	\Delta U(T) = U_{\mathrm{NS}}(T) - U_{\mathrm{N}}(T),
	\label{eq:utility_gap_ch73}
\end{equation}
where $U_{\mathrm{NS}}(T)$ denotes cumulative utility after ASP refinement and $U_{\mathrm{N}}(T)$ denotes cumulative utility from the raw neural anomaly stream. A positive value of $\Delta U(T)$ indicates that symbolic refinement improves the downstream economic value of anomaly detection.

\subsection{Cumulative Utility Over Time}

The most direct way to assess financial usefulness is to inspect cumulative utility over the full evaluation horizon. The repository includes a dedicated plotting script, \texttt{src/23\_plot\_utility\_comparison.py}, which produces a temporal comparison of utility accumulation over time \cite{repo_utility_comparison}. This figure is important because it shows whether the advantage of the refined signals is stable, temporary, or concentrated in specific periods of the year.

If the neuro-symbolic curve remains consistently above the raw neural curve, this indicates that the ASP refinement does more than remove anomalies: it improves the decision value of the retained signals. If the refined curve also exhibits fewer sharp downward movements, that would suggest that symbolic filtering successfully removes penalty-heavy false positives. In practical terms, a smoother and higher cumulative utility trajectory would mean that the refined anomaly stream is not only more selective, but more economically reliable.

\subsection{Neural versus Neuro-Symbolic Utility Comparison}

While the cumulative curve provides a temporal view, a direct aggregate comparison between the neural and neuro-symbolic variants is also necessary. The key point here is that neuro-symbolic refinement may improve total utility even if it reduces the number of anomaly signals. In other words, the framework is not optimized for signal quantity, but for signal quality. A smaller signal set can still be superior if the removed anomalies are mostly penalty-generating false positives. This is exactly the expected behaviour of the proposed framework: retain anomalies that are persistent, plausible, and contextually supported, while removing those that are likely to reduce downstream value.

\subsection{Utility and Penalty Summary}

For precise reporting, the finance-aware backtest should also be summarized in tabular form. \subsection{Interpretation of the Financial Results}

The main interpretation of this section is that anomaly refinement should be judged not only by detection counts, but also by its downstream utility--risk trade-off. A raw detector can produce many alerts, and in some cases those alerts may yield higher cumulative return simply because the strategy acts more often. However, if a substantial portion of those alerts are implausible or weakly supported, then the downstream system pays for that over-sensitivity through additional penalty accumulation. The ASP layer changes this balance by filtering the anomaly stream before it reaches the finance-aware evaluation stage.

This leads to an important conceptual point. In the present result snapshot, symbolic reasoning should not be interpreted as a direct profit-maximizing mechanism. The utility comparison shows that the purely neural strategy achieves the higher cumulative utility and final net profit, whereas the neuro-symbolic strategy yields a lower overall return. At the same time, the refined neuro-symbolic strategy reduces the penalty burden, with the comparison figure indicating a risk reduction of approximately EUR~1{,}432. Thus, the benefit of the symbolic layer is twofold in a more qualified sense:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item it improves anomaly credibility by reducing false positives and weakly supported detections, and
	\item it improves the risk profile of the accepted anomaly stream by reducing penalty-bearing signals.
\end{enumerate}

Accordingly, the contribution of the ASP layer in this experiment is not that it maximizes total simulated financial return, but that it makes anomaly decisions more selective and operationally conservative. In other words, symbolic refinement improves the quality and economic defensibility of accepted anomaly signals, even though this refinement comes at the cost of lower aggregate utility relative to the purely neural strategy.

\subsection{Synthesis}

Section~7.2 showed that symbolic refinement reduces the raw anomaly burden by vetoing implausible or weakly supported detections. Section~7.3 then examined whether the retained anomalies are also more valuable in a downstream financial setting. The finance-aware evaluation shows that the answer is qualified: the neuro-symbolically refined signal stream does not outperform the raw neural strategy in cumulative utility or final net profit, but it does reduce the penalty burden and thereby improves the risk profile of the anomaly stream.

This section is therefore the practical bridge between anomaly quality and decision value. Forecasting residuals generate candidate anomalies, symbolic reasoning filters those candidates according to plausibility and contextual support, and the financial analysis reveals the operational consequences of that filtering. In the present result snapshot, the ASP layer should be interpreted as a risk-mitigating refinement mechanism rather than a profit-maximizing one. The contribution of the neuro-symbolic layer is thus to produce a more selective and economically defensible anomaly stream, even though this refinement reduces aggregate simulated financial return relative to the purely neural strategy.

\section{Case Studies of High-Severity Events}
\label{sec:case_studies_high_severity_events}

Aggregate metrics are useful for assessing overall forecasting behaviour, anomaly burden, and utility trends, but they cannot fully reveal how the proposed neuro-symbolic framework behaves on concrete real-world events. For that reason, this section presents a set of focused case studies drawn from the event-level anomaly table generated by the repository pipeline. The purpose is to move from global summary to event-level interpretation and to show how forecasting error, anomaly persistence, ASP-based refinement, and downstream utility interact in specific high-severity situations.

The case studies are based on the event table \texttt{reports/tables/anomaly\_events\_2022.csv}, which is generated from the refined anomaly stream using the event-building script \texttt{src/10\_build\_event\_table.py} \cite{repo_anomaly_events_2022,repo_build_event_table}. In this pipeline, contiguous anomaly intervals are grouped into events and described using structured event attributes such as \texttt{event\_id}, \texttt{signal\_id}, \texttt{start\_ts}, \texttt{end\_ts}, \texttt{duration\_hours}, \texttt{n\_points}, \texttt{peak\_score}, \texttt{mean\_score}, and \texttt{severity\_label}. This event abstraction is important because it allows anomalies to be interpreted as meaningful temporal episodes rather than as isolated point detections.

For the present section, four representative high-severity events are selected:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item one \textbf{price event},
	\item one \textbf{load event},
	\item one \textbf{renewable event},
	\item one \textbf{multi-signal event}.
\end{enumerate}
This design ensures that the case-study analysis spans market behaviour, demand-side behaviour, renewable-side behaviour, and cross-signal system stress.

\subsection{Selection Criteria}

The selected events were chosen from the repository event table using three practical criteria. First, the event had to be labeled \texttt{HIGH} severity. Second, it had to exhibit a sufficiently large \texttt{peak\_score} to justify focused interpretation. Third, preference was given to events with either longer duration or clearer narrative value, since such events are easier to interpret in a thesis context than very short single-point spikes.

\subsection{Cross-Case Interpretation}

Taken together, the four selected events reveal several important properties of the full framework.

First, anomaly severity is highly heterogeneous across targets. The selected price and wind events are both persistent and extreme, indicating that some of the most important system disturbances are prolonged rather than isolated. By contrast, the selected load event shows that even short events can still be highly severe and worthy of detailed interpretation.

Second, the case studies reinforce the value of event-level abstraction. A simple point-anomaly view would treat each flagged timestamp independently, whereas the event table reveals duration, persistence, and internal severity structure. This makes the resulting anomaly analysis far more interpretable and much better suited to narrative discussion in a thesis.

Third, the multi-signal event confirms that the anomaly framework is not merely a collection of four separate univariate detectors. It is capable of surfacing periods of cross-target irregularity, which are arguably the most relevant situations from an operational point of view.

Finally, the case studies provide the strongest qualitative evidence for the overall thesis claim. The forecasting layer alone produces residuals; the anomaly layer converts those residuals into candidate signals; the symbolic layer refines them; and the finance-aware layer interprets their usefulness. In the case-study view, these layers can be seen working together on real episodes rather than only through aggregate metrics.

\subsection{Synthesis}

Taken together, the selected case studies demonstrate that the proposed framework is capable of capturing qualitatively different types of high-severity anomaly episodes, including sustained market-price disturbances, short but intense load deviations, prolonged renewable-side anomalies, and explicitly multivariate cross-signal events. This is an important result because it shows that the system is not limited to one anomaly archetype, but can represent a broader spectrum of operationally meaningful events.

The event-level analysis also clarifies the value of the neuro-symbolic design. The forecasting layer provides the residual structure that exposes candidate deviations, but the symbolic refinement layer is what makes the final anomaly interpretation more credible and more selective. In the examined cases, the framework supports a richer interpretation of anomalies by combining temporal persistence, signal-specific context, and downstream utility information rather than relying on raw residual magnitude alone.

More broadly, these case studies complement the aggregate findings of Sections~7.1--7.3. While the earlier sections established the statistical, symbolic, and utility-oriented behaviour of the framework at a global level, the present section shows how those properties manifest in concrete event windows. As a result, the case-study analysis provides the most intuitive demonstration of the thesis claim that anomaly detection in electricity systems should be not only data-driven, but also context-aware, interpretable, and operationally meaningful.
